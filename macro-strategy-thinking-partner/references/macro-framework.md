# Macro Framework

## Purpose

Use this framework to convert noisy transcript material into a stable macro-investing reasoning process.

## A. Start from Regime, Not from Tickers

Always identify the current regime first:

1. Growth direction: improving, weakening, or stabilizing
2. Inflation / deflation pressure
3. Liquidity condition and credit impulse
4. Policy intensity and policy credibility
5. Risk appetite and market positioning

If regime is unclear, say so and frame the answer as scenarios instead of certainty.

## B. Policy Is the Main Transmission Hub

The source corpus repeatedly points to one pattern:

- Many themes do not sustain without policy catalyst.
- Liquidity and policy protection matter more than bottom-up storytelling during fragile markets.
- When markets are in a defensive or "financial protection" state, industry selection often becomes secondary to policy support and system stabilization.

So reason in this order:

1. What problem is policy trying to solve now?
2. What tools are available: fiscal, monetary, industrial, regulatory, diplomatic?
3. Which sectors receive first-order benefit?
4. Which sectors only get sentiment spillover?

## C. Industry Thinking Must Follow Transmission Chains

Map each theme from macro to industry:

1. Policy signal
2. Funding / liquidity support
3. Order or demand confirmation
4. Earnings visibility
5. Valuation rerating or derating

Do not treat a theme as "dead" just because price fell. The corpus repeatedly reflects a staged view: a theme can remain structurally alive while being tactically weak.

## D. Distilled Themes from the Current Corpus

### 1. Technology / AI / Digital Economy

- Technology often becomes a core focus when valuations have been heavily compressed.
- AI and digital-economy themes need more than excitement; they need policy continuity, capital support, and demand confirmation.
- "No sustained hotspot without policy catalyst" is a recurring message in the corpus.
- Watch whether the market is getting liquidity plus planning visibility, not just concept rotation.

### 2. Healthcare

- In defensive markets, even good sectors may fail to trend unless the market's macro pressure eases.
- Healthcare should be judged as both a policy industry and a risk-off / quality destination.
- Ask whether the environment is rewarding earnings stability, innovation, or simple liquidity beta.

### 3. Insurance / Wealth Stage / Asset Allocation

- Insurance is not framed as a daily hot topic; it is framed as a wealth-stage allocation tool.
- A recurring pattern is that as household wealth and per-capita GDP cross certain stages, protection and long-duration allocation tools become more relevant.
- Use insurance discussions not only as product talk, but as a clue about household-balance-sheet evolution and asset-allocation maturity.

### 4. Quant and Execution Tools

- Quant belongs mostly to the execution layer, not the thesis layer.
- Use quant to improve implementation, monitoring, or discipline after the macro and industry view is formed.
- Do not let tools replace regime judgment.

## E. Watch Variables

When answering the user, prefer variables like these:

- Policy meetings, wording shifts, and implementation intensity
- Credit expansion or contraction
- Long-end rate direction
- Northbound / foreign-flow behavior when relevant
- Market turnover and liquidity depth
- Whether a sector has real demand confirmation or only narrative heat

## F. Invalidation Rules

Always state what would break the thesis:

- Policy less supportive than expected
- Liquidity not following policy rhetoric
- Demand/order data failing to confirm
- Valuation already pricing the good news
- Theme rotation without follow-through

## G. Output Shape

When asked a macro question, prefer this answer shape:

1. My current base case
2. The policy and macro chain behind it
3. The industry winners / laggards if the base case holds
4. The variables I would watch next
5. What would make me change my mind
